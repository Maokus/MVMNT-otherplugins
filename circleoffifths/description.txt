The video uses a circular, colour-coded map of the twelve chromatic scale degrees to represent tonal relationships. Rather than arranging notes from low to high as on a keyboard, the degrees are positioned according to the circle of fifths. The tonic, labelled 1, is placed at the top as the perceptual “home” of the key, while the remaining degrees are distributed around the circumference. Each degree is assigned a consistent colour and marked by a small circular node.

When a scale degree is being demonstrated, its node becomes visually prominent through an enlarged, glowing crescent or halo extending outside the circle. This allows the viewer to connect the sound being heard with a stable visual identity without translating it into a specific letter name or keyboard position. The speaker remains framed within the centre of the display, so the diagram operates almost like a perceptual field surrounding the listener rather than a conventional chart shown separately from the presentation.

The spatial arrangement is meaningful. Degrees located near one another on the circle are treated as having more closely related “feeling-states,” whereas degrees on opposing sides tend to feel more contrasting. The circle therefore functions as a map of perceived musical similarity, not simply a representation of pitch distance. For example, two notes may be adjacent on a chromatic scale yet appear far apart on the circle because their relationships to the tonic produce very different tonal qualities.

Throughout the video, individual degrees are explored against a continuous tonic drone. The visualisation preserves the tonic as a fixed reference while highlighting the degree currently being heard and compared. This reinforces the central premise of the video: that the character of a note comes not from its absolute pitch, but from its relationship to the tonal centre.

Unlike a conventional audio visualiser, it does not display loudness, frequency spectra or rhythmic energy. It is instead a semantic visualisation of tonal function. Colour distinguishes the twelve identities, position expresses relationships between them, and animated emphasis directs attention to the current musical example. In this way, the system turns the otherwise abstract and subjective idea of musical “feeling” into a persistent spatial structure that can be seen, heard and gradually memorised.
